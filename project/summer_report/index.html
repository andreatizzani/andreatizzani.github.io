Ballots and Battles: Civic Capital in the Shadow of WWI

Jun 30, 2023

How do wartime experiences shape civic capital? Investigating the aftermath of World War I in Italy, I uncover the profound impact of this global conflict on civic capital, measured by voter turnout, in Italian municipalities. I employ an empirical strategy and a theoretical model to study the dynamics of this phenomenon. My empirical strategy employs an event-study design, allowing me to quantify the effects of World War I on voter turnout at each national election between 1886 and 1921. On average, the war led to a 10.1% increase in turnout in municipalities that suffered a higher number of military fatalities due to the conflict. This effect consists of both a positive anticipatory effect observed at the 1913 elections and a positive post-event effect. Interestingly, the results reveal a stark regional contrast: Southern municipalities anticipate more with no significant effect post- war, while the North shows the opposite pattern. To shed light on this heterogeneity, I construct a two-period theoretical model. This model uncovers the dynamic nature of voting habits and offers a possible explanation for the observed regional differences. Overall, this study delivers a comprehensive understanding of political behavior in times of crisis, revealing the profound and diverse impacts of the conflict on civic capital.
